6 Common Programing Paradigm's

With a PB and J sandwich

February 20, 2016

Whats a programing paradigm you ask... Well in one sense of the meaning its a style/way of structuring a program. In another it is a way to classify a programing language given its features and execution.

  • Imperative Programing

    Is a programing paradigm that uses statements to change a programs state.

    		•
  • Object-Orientated Programing

    Is a programing paradigm based on the concept of objects. These objects contain data structures that contain data. The data is represented as an attribute of the object. Objects also contain code proceeder that can manipulate the data refereed to in most cases as a method.

    		•
  • Functional Programing

    Is a programing paradigm that calculates mathematical functions while avoiding the change of a program's state.

  • Logic Programing

    A programing paradigm based on formal logic. Typically written as sets of sentences containing proofs and clauses as well as subgoals.

    		•
  • Symbolic Programing

    Allows for the manipulation of its own program giving it the effect and appearance of learning. This style is typically used for AI's and games.

    		•
  • Procedural Programing

    This is a breakaway of Imperative given the addition of creating and calling functions.

    		•

    To clear some stuff up before we move on: A computer stores data called variables. Some variables are subject to change and their fore refereed to as mutable while others are immutable and not be changed for a number of given reasons. The contents of the variables at any given time represent the state.

    Peanut Butter Jelly Time!

    When I tell you to make a "PB&J" you make a peanut butter jelly sandwich. When we tell a computer to make a pb&j we get this a hand full of error messages and and a couple headaches later we have an idea of of what a pb&j. Lets take a look at the many different paradigms of a PB&J. FYI you may have caught on to this if not thats okay I'm telling you now, some languages fall into many different paradigms. At that point it is up to the discretion of the writer as to which paradigms are in play. So here we go! Lets make a peanut butter jelly sandwich and maybe even score a glass of milk. Code examples:

    Starting simple and keeping it simple PB&J with Imperative (python):

    
   #Make the ingredients
  bread_loaf = ["bread", "bread", "bread", "bread", "bread", "bread"]
  pb = "Creamy smooth peanut butter just how i like it"
  add = "with"
  jam = "strawberry jam"
  #assemble the sandwich
  sandwich = [bread_loaf.pop(), add, jam, add, pb, bread_loaf.pop()]
  for make, serve in enumerate(sandwich):
      print serve # me a sandwich

    Thats pretty simple right . You created variables changed the state of bread_loaf by taking two pieces from it changing the state of bread loaf.
